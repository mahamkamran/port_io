3D Coupled Vortex-Odor Dynamics

3D Computational Investigations of Integrated Vortex-Odor Dynamics in the Wake of Fish for Underwater Sensing

Maham Kamran*, C. Li, and MSU Khalid

MSc. Thesis Research @ Nature Inspired Engineering Research Lab (NIERL), Lakehead University, Canada
Supervisor: Dr. Muhammad Saif Ullah Khalid, P.Eng
Research Collaboration with Case Wesetern Reserve University, USA: Dr. Chengyu Li

In this research, the three-dimensional (3D) Integrated Vortex-Odor Dynamics in the Wake of Fish for Underwater Sensing was examined, focusing on bio-inspired multi-physics sensing mechanisms. Utilizing computational fluid dynamics (CFD) based simulations with an in-house Immersed-Boundary Method (IBM) solver, VorteXdyn, the investigation centered on how different waveform patterns, specifically carangiform and anguilliform, influence the dispersion of chemical cues in fluidic environments. For this work, high-fidelity three-dimensional simulations were conducted for fluid-structure-chemical interactions around real fish-like bodies (Jack Fish, a carangiform swimmer, and American eel, an anguilliform swimmer) to determine the connectivity between vortex dynamics and the transport of chemical cues in the wake of these fish. Additionally, the comparative analysis of the diffusive and advective mechanisms in the transport phenomena in relation to the undulatory kinematics was also explained. Moreover, solitary arrangements as well as parallel schooling configurations were employed to decipher the complex vortex-odor interactions for underwater sensing. This research aims to enhance the design of more efficient, adaptive, and intelligent autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) by integrating sensory and hydrodynamic principles inspired by fish locomotion.

2D Coupled Vortex-Odor Dynamics

How does vortex dynamics help undulating bodies spread odor? [ Publication DOI: 10.1063/5.0235768]

Maham Kamran*, A. Fardi, C. Li, and MSU Khalid

MSc. Thesis Research @ Nature Inspired Engineering Research Lab (NIERL), Lakehead University, Canada
Supervisor: Dr. Muhammad Saif Ullah Khalid, P.Eng
Research Collaboration with Case Wesetern Reserve University, USA: Dr. Chengyu Li

In this paper, we examine the coupling between odor dynamics and vortex dynamics around undulating bodies, with a focus on bio-inspired propulsion mechanisms. Utilizing computational fluid dynamics simulations with an in-house immersed boundary method solver, we investigate how different waveform patterns, specifically carangiform and anguilliform, influence the dispersion of chemical cues in both water and air environments. Our findings reveal that vortex dynamics significantly impact the overall trajectory of odor spots, although the alignment between odor spots and coherent flow structures is not always precise. We also evaluate the relative contributions of diffusion and convection in odor transport, showing that convection dominates in water, driven by higher Schmidt numbers, while diffusion plays a more prominent role alongside convection in air. Additionally, the anguilliform waveform generally produces stronger and farther-reaching chemical cues compared to carangiform swimmers. The critical roles of Strouhal number and Reynolds number in determining the efficiency of odor dispersion are also explained, offering insights that could enhance the design of more efficient, adaptive, and intelligent autonomous underwater vehicles by integrating sensory and hydrodynamic principles inspired by fish locomotion.

Aerothermal Design Analysis of Aircraft

Aerothermal Design Analysis of Dorsal Mounted Avionics LRU in Unconditioned Bay

Maham Kamran*, Dr. Ali Sarosh

BASc. Thesis Research @ Shock and Stars Pvt Ltd, Air University, Pakistan
Supervisor: Dr. Ali Sarosh

Airworthiness of avionics in fighter aircraft is challenging due to drastic environmental changes and strict military standards. Modern aircraft feature complex avionics systems with thermal management issues, especially in unconditioned environments. The thermal evaluation of Software-Defined Radio (SDR) follows MIL-E-5400 standards. This research uses a two-step analytical approach combined with CFD to assess the thermal regimes of an indigenously developed LRU. The first step involves lumped mass analysis to determine temperature regimes, while the second step analyzes temperature variations with altitude and Mach number. CFD simulates both temperature distribution and airflow for cooling efficiency. Results show that temperature decreases with altitude up to the troposphere but increases beyond it due to reduced heat transfer properties. Hot spots on the SDR and enclosure walls are also identified.
